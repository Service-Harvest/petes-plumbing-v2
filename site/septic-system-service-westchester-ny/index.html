The warnings that come before a backup

Plenty of homes across this county are on septic rather than municipal sewer, sometimes on the same road as neighbours who aren’t. The good news is that septic systems rarely fail without notice. The bad news is that the early signs are easy to explain away.

These are the ones worth acting on:

  • Everything slows down at once. Not one fixture — the whole house. On septic this points at the tank or the line to it rather than a clog.
  • Gurgling after normal use. Toilets and tubs burbling when a washing machine drains means the system is struggling to take the volume.
  • It’s worse after heavy rain. A drain field that’s already near capacity can’t accept more when the ground around it is saturated. That seasonal pattern is a real signal, not a coincidence.
  • Unusually green, lush grass over the tank or field. Growth that outpaces the rest of the lawn means something is fertilising it.
  • Soft ground or standing water anywhere over the system, in dry weather.
  • Smell outside near the tank rather than inside the house.

Any one of those on its own may be nothing. Two or three together, especially with the rain pattern, is a system telling you it needs attention while attention is still affordable.

What goes down the drain matters more here

A septic tank works by giving solids time to settle and bacteria time to break them down. Anything that kills the bacteria or won’t break down shortens the system’s life, and unlike a municipal sewer, the consequences land in your own yard and on your own bill.

Worth keeping out of the system:

  • Wipes of any kind, including the ones labelled flushable. They clear the toilet and then sit in the tank indefinitely.
  • Grease. It floats, builds a layer, and eventually carries over into the drain field, where it clogs the soil itself. That failure is expensive and not really repairable.
  • Chemical drain cleaners. They don’t distinguish between a clog and the bacteria your tank depends on.
  • Coffee grounds, cat litter, and heavy paper products. All settle as solids that only pumping will remove.
  • Large volumes at once. Several loads of laundry back to back push water through faster than solids can settle. Spreading them across the week genuinely helps.

One more thing worth knowing: never route a sump pump or gutter discharge into a septic system. Groundwater floods the tank and field and can cause a much more expensive failure than the one you were trying to solve. The EPA’s homeowner guidance on caring for a septic system covers the maintenance side in plain language and is worth a read if you’ve recently moved into a house with one.

Which part of this is a plumber’s job

Septic work splits across two trades, and being clear about that saves you a wasted visit.

Ours is the plumbing side: the waste lines inside the house, the line running out to the tank, diagnosing whether a whole-house slowdown is a blockage or the system itself, and the fixture and drain work that connects to it. When the problem is between your house and the tank, that’s septic tank backups and line trouble and it’s work we do.

Not ours is tank pumping, drain field replacement, and system design or installation. Those are septic contractors with different equipment and different permits. We’ll tell you when that’s what you need rather than charging you to find out slowly.

The reason it’s worth calling a plumber first is that the symptoms overlap almost completely. A blocked line to the tank and a full tank produce the same slow drains. One is a straightforward repair; the other is a pumping bill or worse. Working out which you’re looking at is quick, and it means you call the right trade once. It’s the same reasoning behind how we approach general drain and sewer services — find out what it actually is before committing anyone to work. What affects the cost

Because we handle diagnosis and the plumbing side, the cost depends on what we find. Establishing whether the problem is the tank, the line, or the house plumbing is the first and cheapest step, and it decides everything after.

A blocked or broken line between house and tank is priced like any buried line. We would always rather rule out the inexpensive causes — a filter, a simple blockage — before anyone talks about larger work or the wrong trade is called.

Common questions

How often should a septic tank be pumped?

It depends on tank size and household size, and it’s a septic contractor’s call rather than ours. What we’d say is that pumping on a sensible schedule costs far less than the drain field damage that follows from leaving it too long.

Do you pump septic tanks?

No — that’s specialist equipment and a different trade. We handle the plumbing side: the lines, the diagnosis, and the work inside the house. If pumping is what you need, we’ll say so.

How do I know if I’m on septic at all?

If there’s no sewer charge from your municipality and there’s a lid or inspection cover somewhere in the yard, you’re almost certainly on septic. Buying a house and not sure? It should be on the property records, and it’s worth confirming before closing.

Are septic tank additives worth buying?

Generally not. A working tank already has the bacteria it needs, and no additive removes accumulated solids — only pumping does that. Money is better spent on regular pumping and on keeping the wrong things out in the first place.

My drains are slow but only when it rains. Is that the septic?

Very possibly. A saturated drain field can’t accept more water, so the system backs up until the ground drains. It can also mean groundwater is getting into the line through a crack. Either way it tends to worsen rather than settle.
