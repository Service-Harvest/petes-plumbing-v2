What counts as fixture replacement

Fixtures are the parts of the plumbing you actually touch and see. When one is worn, dated, or failing, replacing it is usually a modest job with a big visible improvement. This covers:

  • Faucets, taps, and mixers, indoors and out.
  • Shut-off and supply valves, especially old ones that no longer fully close.
  • Showerheads, hand showers, and diverters.
  • Toilet fill and flush mechanisms, seats, and supply lines.
  • Traps, tailpieces, and the visible waste connections under a sink.
  • Sink and tub drain assemblies, pop-ups, and stoppers.

Some overlap with their own dedicated jobs — a full sink installation is more than a fixture swap, for instance. But the small stuff, the bits that wear out and date a room, is exactly what this covers.

Why old houses complicate a simple swap

Here is the honest version of why a “five-minute” fixture swap sometimes is not, and it matters in this county because so much of the housing stock is not new.

The fixture itself comes off easily. The trouble is what it is attached to. A shut-off valve that has not been turned in twenty years often will not fully close, or starts weeping the moment it is disturbed. A supply connection can be corroded solid. A tailpiece can crumble when touched. None of this is anyone doing anything wrong — it is simply old metal reaching the end of its life the moment it is asked to move.

A plumber who does this regularly expects it, shuts off further back when a local valve fails, and carries the parts to replace what breaks. It is also why we would rather look before quoting a firm number on an older fixture — the swap is predictable, but what is behind it is not always. If a supply line turns out to be failing rather than just the fixture, that is a pipe repair rather than a fixture swap, and we will tell you that before proceeding rather than after.

Doing several at once

Fixtures are one area where batching genuinely saves money. Much of the cost of a single fixture visit is the visit itself, so replacing several in one appointment spreads that across all of them.

If you are refreshing a bathroom or kitchen without a full renovation, a round of fixture replacement — faucets, showerhead, toilet mechanism, valves, trap — often transforms how a room feels and works for a fraction of a remodel. And while everything is apart, the old shut-off valves are best replaced at the same time, so the next job in that spot is not fighting a seized valve.

Common questions

Can I supply the fixtures and have you fit them?

Yes, and many people do. It is worth a quick check that what you have bought suits the existing connections and layout — particularly with older plumbing, where fittings are not always what a modern fixture expects.

Why might a simple swap cost more than expected?

Almost always because of what the fixture is attached to — a seized or weeping shut-off valve, a corroded supply line, or a crumbling tailpiece. We would rather replace those than reconnect to something about to fail.

Should I replace shut-off valves even if they seem fine?

If they are old and you are already in there, it is cheap insurance. An old valve that half-closes turns the next small job into a bigger one, because you cannot isolate the fixture cleanly.

Is replacing a fixture worth it, or should I renovate?

For dated but functional rooms, fixture replacement gives a lot of the visual lift of a remodel at a fraction of the cost and disruption. A full renovation only makes sense when you are changing the layout or the surfaces too.

How many can you do in one visit?

Several, usually — that is the efficient way to do it. Give us the full list when booking so we arrive with everything the job needs.
