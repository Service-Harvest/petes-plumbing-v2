What fails, and what it tells you

The material tells you a lot about what is happening and what to expect next. Houses around here contain a real mixture, often several types in the same building from decades of partial work.

Copper is the most common supply material in this area and generally excellent. It fails through pinhole corrosion from the inside, at joints where the workmanship was poor, or where it has been rubbing against a joist for forty years.

Galvanised steel in older houses corrodes internally, narrowing the bore and eventually restricting flow badly. A galvanised system usually announces itself through falling pressure and rusty water before it leaks.

Polybutylene, used in some construction of a certain era, is known to fail unpredictably. If you have grey plastic supply pipe with crimped fittings, it is worth having identified properly.

Cast iron waste scales up internally and eventually rusts through, most often at the base of a stack where it has been wet longest.

PEX and modern plastics are durable, and failures are usually at fittings or from physical damage rather than the pipe itself.

Why pinholes come in groups

This is the single most useful thing we can tell you about copper pipe, and it is the reason we ask about history before quoting.

Pinhole corrosion is caused by conditions inside the pipe — water chemistry, flow velocity, temperature, and how the system was installed. Those conditions apply along the whole run, not to one inch of it. So when a pipe develops a pinhole, the rest of that run has been living through exactly the same conditions for exactly as long.

In practice that means a first pinhole is often followed by a second somewhere else on the same line, then a third. Each one individually looks like a small repair. Collectively they add up to more than replacing the run would have cost, plus several separate episodes of water damage.

So the honest conversation after a pinhole is not just about the patch. It is about whether this is the first, and if it is the third, whether when patching is no longer enough has already arrived. We would rather have that conversation with you than quietly keep coming back.

The PHCC, the national plumbing contractors association, has consumer guidance on working with a plumbing contractor that covers the questions worth asking before agreeing to any significant repair.

How we approach a repair

  1. Stop the water and confirm the source. Water tracks along pipes and joists, so the visible drip is not always the hole.
  2. Look at the pipe, not just the leak. Condition either side of the failure tells us whether this is isolated or the start of a pattern.
  3. Explain what we found and the options. Usually a repair and a more thorough alternative, with what each realistically buys you.
  4. Price before starting. Always, and re-quoted if opening up changes the picture.
  5. Repair properly. Cut back to sound pipe rather than patching over questionable material, and support the run properly so the repair is not under strain.
  6. Test and clean up. Pressure restored, every joint checked, mess taken with us.

Where a repair is temporary by nature — getting a household through the weekend before a proper fix — we will say so plainly rather than presenting it as finished work.

Common questions

Can you repair a pipe inside a finished wall?

Yes, though it needs access. We locate it precisely first so the opening is one deliberate spot. Sometimes rerouting a short section through an accessible path causes less disruption than reaching the original.

Is a push-fit repair coupling acceptable?

They have their place, particularly for a fast repair in a wet or awkward spot. For permanent work in a concealed location we generally prefer a soldered or pressed joint, and we will tell you which we are using and why.

My water has gone rusty. Is that the pipes?

Often, particularly with galvanised supply lines or an ageing water heater. If it is only hot water, suspect the heater. If it is both, suspect the piping.

Why does my pipe bang when I turn a tap off?

Water hammer — the moving column of water stopping abruptly. It is usually fixable with arrestors or by properly securing loose pipe, and it is worth doing, since repeated shock stresses joints over time.

How urgent is a small drip?

More urgent than it looks if it is inside a wall or above a ceiling, because the damage accumulates invisibly. A visible drip into a basement with an open floor is less pressing, though it still means something is failing.
